Simulations of binary interactions

Transients from stellar collisions

When two stars cross paths and end up colliding, they eject material which will later interact with the radiation from the collision product. These interactions will lead to a brightness increase, i.e. a transient, which I would like to predict. To this end, I first simulate the collision with Phantom, a Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH*) code. Once I obtain the amount of mass ejected, it will be used as input into a radiative hydrodynamics code to study how the radiation emitted by the collision product will interact with the ejected matter.

* SPH is a technique that discretises the hydrodynamic equations by approximating a fluid as a collection of particles. Each particle has its own properties (position, velocity, energy, etc) and one can interpolate the variables of interest (e.g. density) to visualise the behaviour of the fluid(s). While this process has many downsides, it is a good approximation in highly dynamical contexts, like stellar collisions, which are computationally expensive in 3D grid-based Hydrodynamic codes.
